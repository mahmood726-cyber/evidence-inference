When Statistical Corrections Change Everything

A study of 434 meta-analyses reveals that one in eleven published conclusions may be wrong — and shows which ones are most at risk

By Research Team

January 9, 2026

In the world of medical research, meta-analyses are supposed to be the gold standard — combining results from multiple studies to find the truth. But what happens when a simple statistical correction can flip a "significant" finding to "not significant"?

We analyzed 434 Cochrane meta-analyses to find out. The answer: 9.2% of conclusions changed when we applied a correction designed for small samples. That's roughly one in eleven studies where the headline finding may be misleading.

Two Methods, One Question

We compared two approaches that claim to measure meta-analysis reliability:

MA4 Stability Index (R-index) measures how consistent results are when you vary the analysis method. A high score means the finding is stable across different statistical choices.

HKSJ Correction (Hartung-Knapp-Sidik-Jonkman) adjusts confidence intervals for small samples, using a t-distribution instead of a normal distribution. It's more conservative — and sometimes changes conclusions.

"We expected stable meta-analyses to resist correction. We were wrong."

The central question: Does a high stability score protect against conclusion changes when corrections are applied? The answer surprised us.

The Paradox of "Moderate" Stability

When we looked at conclusion change rates by stability category, something unexpected emerged. The highest change rate wasn't in the "Low" stability group — it was in the "Moderate" group.

Key Insight

The "Moderate" category has the highest proportion of initially significant results (58.9%). These are borderline cases — significant under standard analysis, but vulnerable to correction. Among initially significant meta-analyses in the Moderate category, 25% changed conclusions.

The results are striking. The stability index (R) shows no significant effect — its confidence interval spans from 0.14 to 27.5, crossing 1.0 by a wide margin. But one factor stands out:

"Initially significant results are 5.4 times more likely to change conclusions under HKSJ correction."

This makes intuitive sense. The HKSJ correction widens confidence intervals. If a result was barely significant to begin with, the wider interval is likely to cross zero, flipping the conclusion.

What This Means for Research

Three Key Takeaways

  • Stability and correction sensitivity are different things. A high R-index doesn't protect against HKSJ-induced conclusion changes. These metrics measure complementary, not redundant, aspects of robustness.
  • Significant results are vulnerable. If your meta-analysis is barely significant (p close to 0.05), applying HKSJ correction may flip the conclusion. This affects about 15% of initially significant meta-analyses.
  • Report both metrics. For comprehensive quality assessment, meta-analysts should report both the stability index and HKSJ-adjusted results, especially for small meta-analyses.

Methodology

We analyzed 434 meta-analyses from 82 Cochrane reviews, each containing 5 or more studies. For each meta-analysis, we computed the MA4 v1.0.1 stability index (R) and applied the HKSJ correction using the metafor package in R. A "conclusion change" was defined as a switch in statistical significance (p < 0.05 to p ≥ 0.05, or vice versa). Logistic regression was used to identify predictors of conclusion change, with variance inflation factors confirming no multicollinearity (all VIF < 1.2).

Data availability: All code and data are available for reproducibility.

The implications extend beyond methodology. When one in eleven meta-analysis conclusions may change under a more conservative correction, we should ask: how many treatment guidelines are built on findings that wouldn't survive scrutiny?

The answer isn't to distrust all meta-analyses. It's to demand transparency — reporting both standard and corrected results, acknowledging when findings are borderline, and recognizing that "significant" is not the same as "stable."
