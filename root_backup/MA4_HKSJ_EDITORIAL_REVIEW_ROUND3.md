## Strengths of Final Manuscript

### 1. Honest Null Finding
The authors clearly report that R-index is **not significantly associated** with HKSJ conclusion stability (r = 0.05, p = 0.26). This is a valuable negative finding that prevents over-interpretation of MA4 in the context of small-sample adjustments.

### 2. Excellent Paradox Resolution
The non-monotonic pattern is now fully explained:
- "Moderate" R-index contains 58.9% initially significant MAs
- Among initially significant MAs, "Moderate" shows 25% change rate
- This represents borderline cases where HKSJ tips the balance

This explanation is scientifically sound and adds to our understanding of both methods.

### 3. Clinically Actionable Finding
The discovery that **initially significant results are 5.4x more likely to change** under HKSJ is immediately actionable for meta-analysts. This is the key take-home message.

### 4. Appropriate Model Diagnostics
VIF check (all <1.2) confirms the wide CI is due to low event rate, not multicollinearity. This addresses the statistical concern transparently.

### 5. Sensitivity Analysis
Collapsing to 3 categories confirms robustness of findings. The significant chi-square with collapsed categories (p < 0.001) is appropriately explained as driven by Moderate vs Low/Very Low, not by R-index linearly predicting outcome.

### 6. Effect Type Acknowledged
Heterogeneity by effect type (p = 0.025) is properly documented in limitations with direction for future research.

## Impact Statement

This paper makes three important contributions to the meta-analysis literature:

1. **Clarifies the relationship between stability and small-sample correction** - These are complementary, not redundant, concepts.

2. **Identifies the key predictor of HKSJ sensitivity** - Baseline significance, not R-index, determines vulnerability to conclusion change.

3. **Provides practical guidance** - Meta-analysts should report both metrics and exercise caution with small, significant meta-analyses.
